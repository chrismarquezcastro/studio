Mobile apps first major redesign

Kiteworks | Q1 – 2018 | Palo Alto, California

Design Problem

Problems observed

The navigation across the application wasn’t intuitive. For example the main navigation was permanently available in the tablet, but on phones the user needed to navigate all the way up in the tree to get to the menu.

The look and feel was too different from the mental models and interactions used by the operating systems (iOS, Android).

There was no way to understand how and if people were using our products and services.

Hypothesis

There was no need to force the same UI across all platforms. There are plenty of UI patterns and components native to each platform and form factor, there is no need to re-invent everything.

Better design decisions could be made if we could track down the user real behavior as much as possible.

What the stake-holders asked me to solve

This is one of those projects where no one formally asked me to solve anything specifically.

My motivation was driven by listening to the customer support team about customer complaints and some key PM members.

Once I planted the idea and showed some options, the requests started coming in, for example it was during this iteration that the first version of the camera mode hit the market.

How I set up to solve the problem

I explored the market for similar products on the B2C space to identify the simpler and common patters. Specially the native Material Design and iOS components.

Interview external and internal stake-holders to get their perspective on the current state of the app.

The interview also dived into the expectations for the application, in terms of business value to Kiteworks as well as its customers.

Prototype and validate different ideas for navigation, key areas of the product as well as new functionality.

My Role

Collaboration across teams

It was key to socialize the problem among the PM and new executives, get them involved in the problem and the solution.

Partnering with PM and Engineering leads helped me balance the design ideas to meet the crucial business needs while optimizing development.

It was critical to manage the expectations from development and the executives so that progress was made at a good pace.

Frequent check-ins with the customer support team helped to get a perspective from someone who have heard the bad feedback of the previous version.

Facilitation

The Product Manager and I engaged with internal and external stake-holders in a cycle of design reviews and iterations.

The Engineering teams and I identified the best approach to design and develop the necessary components, re-using as much as possible from each native library.

Impact to the team

The new design enabled new workflows like the camera view that will go on to be used more widely in future releases.

Proposed Solution

Use the navigation patterns widely adopted on the native platforms.

The bottom navigation as well as the overlay menu were common patterns in the iOS and Android UI libraries.

For the initial phase the design for tablets was very similar to reduce the complications and hopefuly have a single code base for most components regarless of the devide.

Optimize the navigation every step of the way.

Make the UI so simple that it feels like there is nothing between the user and the content.

The UI should only show when is needed, or at least that's how it should feel like for the end-users, from biometric sign in to seamless gestures.

Redesign every workflow and prioritize the most important use cases.

The email view now includes a badge to show the number of unread messages.

The look and feel should be similar to other commercial apps the do the same thing, to leverage the knowledge most people already have from using these.

Take photos from the app and the content doesn't get saved to the device.

The viewer of files was simplified to show and hide the controls for the convenience of the user.

End to end designer means to go into every situation with a critical point of view to identify the right problems and solve them.

The new camera mode was front and center, readily available for the user's thumbs.

The goal is to add more easy ways to add content into the kiteworks platform.

Every new piece of content added to the platform represents an investment from users to come back to platform in the near future.

How the solution solved the problem

What can the users do now, VS before

End-users can now leverage the design patterns they've learned over time using other mobile apps.

Life improvements

Fewer customers out there with potential exposures, and for those remaining now there is a record of someone manually confirming the setting in case of a dispute.

Challenges

There was turmoil due to the replacement of most of the C suite.

I saw the crisis as an opportunity to propose a new way for the UX design philosophy.

The main change that needed to get across was from a single fixed design across all clients, to the simplicity and leverage of the native platforms.

The design team from then on was integrated by the PM team.

Being close to the PM team can help to gain insights on the business problem from the ground up.

The amount of information can also be challenging due to numerous projects, problems, and overall queries that the PM team receives on a daily basis.

The ratio of PM and Engineers to designers was overwhelming

Simplicity is the best friend when trying to work with multiple teams building in different platforms at the same time.

Communication and a good partnership with the engineering team help to identify the components that can be used out of the box, and those that need modifications to fit within the style.

Impact

Customer and Users

A better, simpler, more performant product will always be good for the customer.

Kiteworks organization

Happier customers are more engaged and less likely to drop the product from the vendor list.

Lessons Learned

Personally

Times of crisis are not as bad as they seem, and are actually times of opportunity.

As a team

A new team was born from here on, The PM-UX with 80% of PM and only 20% of Designers. It kind of worked.
